Indian Poetics: Function and Features of Poetry, Purpose of Poetry, The Word and Meaning, Rasa, Dhwani, Alankar, Vakrokti.

  • INDIAN-POETICS
  • Also known as Kavya Shastra an artistic contemptation.
  • It delves into the essence of poetry, its function,purpose,and the intricate dance between word and meaning.
  • It offers a unique perspective on the art of creating and appreciating poetry,
  • Emphasizing its ability to evoke emotions, convey profound meaning, and elevate the soul.
  • Function and Features of Poetry
  • Dharma:- uplifts soul and imports moral values, guiding towards righteous living and spiritual liberation
  • Artha:- Provide knowledge and wisdom,enriching the mind and understanding the world.
  • Karma:- Poetry offered aesthetic pleasure delighting the senses and evoking emotions
  • Moksha:- Ultimately, it aimed to elevate the soul and lead to spiritual liberation
  • Purpose of Poetry:-
  • Ananda:-Bliss or joy
  • Rasa(Aesthetic Experience):- Heightened emotions through its imagery, language and rhythm.
  • Bodha(Awakening)
  • Sanskriti(Refinement)
  • Samshodhana(Refining Emotions)
  • Samaya(communication)
  • Purpose of Poetry:- Function: Unlike some Western theories that focus on instruction or catharsis(catharsis refers to the emotional release experienced by an audience.), Indian poetics view poetry primarily as a means of aesthetic pleasure (Rasa) and spiritual upliftment. Features: Poetry achieves this through various features, including:-
  • Evocative Language: Words are carefully chosen for their sound, imagery, and ability to suggest emotions and ideas beyond the literal meaning.
  • Figurative Language: Similes, metaphors, and other figures of speech create vivid imagery and add depth to the poem.
  • Rhythmic Patterns: Meter and rhyme schemes create a musicality that enhances the poem's impact.
  • Structure: The overall structure of the poem, with its stanzas and organization, contributes to the flow and development of ideas.
  • The Word and Meaning:-
  • shabda(word):-sound,rhythm
  • Artha(meaning)
  • Dhvanyalokana:-(theory of suggestion)
  • shabda Brahman
  • sphota theory
  • Alankara (figures of speech)
  • Indian poetics emphasizes the importance of the interplay between shabda (word) and artha (meaning). Words are not mere vessels for meaning; their sound and form also contribute to the overall experience. The concept of dhvani (suggestion) further elaborates on this. A poem's true meaning often lies not just in the literal words but in what they suggest or evoke in the reader's mind.
  • Rasa(Aesthetic Experience)
  • It refers to the complex emotional state
  • A state that trancends mere intellectual understanding.
  • Nine(9) Primary Rasas are:-
  • Sringar Rasa (Love)
  • Hasya Rasa(Laughter)
  • Karuna Rasa(Pathos)
  • Raudra Rasa(Anger)
  • Vira Rasa(Heroism)
  • Bhayanakara Rasa(Fear)
  • Vibhatsa Rasa(Disgust)
  • Adbhuta Rasa(wonder)
  • Shanta Rasa(Peace)
  • Rasa(emotions presented love, anger, compassion, heroism, etc. ):- The essence of Indian poetics, Rasa refers to the aesthetic experience or emotional response evoked in the reader by the poem. It's not simply about conveying emotions but creating a space where the reader can temporarily identify with the emotions presented.Different schools of thought propose various Rasas, with the most common being love, anger, compassion, heroism, etc.
  • Dhwani:-
  • Beyond the literal meaning of words.
  • Suggested meaning can be more powerful and nuanced than the literal meaning.
  • Adds depth and complexity to poetry
  • Dhwani:- As mentioned earlier, Dhwani refers to the suggestive or implied meaning in a poem. It's the underlying layer that goes beyond the literal and creates the true impact of the Rasa.
  • Alankar(Figures of speech)
  • used to enhance the beauty,clarity, and impact of the poem.
  • used to create vivid imagery, evoke emotions,emphasize ideas and play with language in creative ways.
  • Some common Alankaras:-
  • Alankar(metaphors, similes, personification, hyperbole, etc.):-
    metaphors ( direct comparison of two things that aren't alike, but share some important characteristic.)
  • similes (comparison of similarities )
  • personification (Personification is a figure of speech that breathes life into things that aren't alive! It does this by giving human characteristics to objects, ideas, or animals.)
  • hyperbole(extreme exaggeration)
  • Alankara refers to figures of speech or ornamentation used in poetry. These include metaphors, similes, personification, hyperbole, etc. While not the sole focus, Alankara adds beauty, depth, and complexity to the poem.
  • Vakrokti(Twisting of Words):-
  • Refers to the clever and unexpected use of words
  • to create surprise,humor and new insights
  • use to challenge conventions,and reveal hidden meanings by using:-
  • Irony
  • Paradox
  • Ambiguity
  • Vakrokti("saying things in a twisted way."):- This concept emphasizes the importance of "saying things in a twisted way." It's not about being obscure but using language creatively and with a twist to convey a deeper meaning or surprise the reader.
