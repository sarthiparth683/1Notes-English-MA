UNIT II Shudrak: Mrichchkatikam tr. M M Ramachandra Kale (New Delhi: Motilal Banarasidas, 1962)

    Shudraka's Mrichchkatikam (The Little Clay Cart) and their characteristics:
  • Charudatta: The protagonist, a Brahmin known for his generosity and good character.
  • Vasantasena: A beautiful and independent courtesan who falls in love with Charudatta.
  • Samsthanaka: The antagonist, the brother-in-law of the king, who is arrogant and lusts after Vasantasena.
  • Rohini: Charudatta's wife.
  • Arthashastra: Charudatta's young son.
  • Vita: Charudatta's friend, a gambler and drunkard, but ultimately loyal.
  • Radanika: Vasantasena's maid, a confidante and voice of reason.
  • Shyama: A servant girl who plays a crucial role in the plot.
  • Vasumitra: A goldsmith who becomes entangled in the story.
  • Plava: The king, portrayed as a weak and easily manipulated ruler.
    Summary of Mrichchhakatika(The Little Clay Cart)
  • Act 1: Down on His Luck We meet Charudatta, a once-wealthy Brahmin merchant known for his generosity. Now fallen on hard times, he laments his financial woes. A poor Brahmin arrives, secretly a messenger for Vasantasena, a renowned courtesan. Despite his own struggles, Charudatta shares his meager meal. Charudatta's compassion sets the stage for their intertwined destinies. Later, Charudatta stumbles upon a valuable lost necklace. His inherent honesty compels him to find the owner and return it.
  • Act 2: A Chance Encounter and Blossoming Love On his quest to return the necklace, Charudatta encounters Vasantasena, who has been attacked by Samsthanaka, a powerful and disreputable nobleman. Charudatta helps her escape and becomes smitten by her beauty and wit. Vasantasena, impressed by Charudatta's nobility, reciprocates his feelings. However, she acknowledges the disparity in their social standings, causing tension.
  • Act 3: A Web of Desire and Intrigue Samsthanaka, enraged by Vasantasena's rejection of his advances, plots to have her abducted. A comedic subplot involving a mischievous servant and a stolen ring adds to the chaos. Charudatta, unaware of Samsthanaka's scheme, encounters Vasantasena again. This time, she seeks his protection, having escaped another attack. Vasantasena, fearing further threats, entrusts Charudatta with her prized possessions, including the necklace he previously found.
  • Act 4: A Tragic Twist and False Accusations Charudatta's young son, playing nearby, unknowingly uses the necklace to adorn his clay cart. The distraught child loses the necklace while playing. Meanwhile, Samsthanaka, in a fit of rage, murders a servant girl and stages it to look like Vasantasena's demise. He plants the stolen necklace near Charudatta's house, framing him for the murder. Charudatta is arrested and imprisoned, heartbroken and confused. Vasantasena vanishes, presumed dead.
  • Act 5: A Cowherd with a Destiny and a Twist of Fate We meet Aryaka, a seemingly ordinary cowherd, who reveals a secret past. A prophecy foretold his destined rise to the throne. Aryaka's arrival coincides with a rebellion against the tyrannical King Palaka. The rebels see Aryaka as their leader. In prison, Charudatta befriends a Buddhist monk who had earlier rescued Vasantasena. The monk reveals Vasantasena is alive but hiding, fearing for her safety.
  • Act 6: Unveiling the Truth and Justice Prevails The rebellion gains momentum, and Aryaka leads them to victory. King Palaka is overthrown, and Aryaka ascends the throne. Vasantasena, seeing the change in power, emerges from hiding. Aryaka recognizes the necklace found near Charudatta and reveals its true owner. Charudatta is released, cleared of all charges. Samsthanaka's crimes are exposed, and he faces punishment under the new king.
  • Act 7: A Happy Ending and Renewed Hope Charudatta and Vasantasena, finally free from societal barriers, are reunited. Their love story triumphs. Aryaka, the new king, promises a just and prosperous reign, fulfilling the prophecy.
  • Additional Details The play delves deeper into themes of social class, the power of love, and the triumph of justice over injustice. It explores the complex relationship between Charudatta and Vasantasena, navigating societal expectations and personal desires. Shudraka infuses humor through the subplot involving the mischievous servant, offering comic relief amidst the dramatic events. The play's ending leaves a sense of hope for a brighter future under King Aryaka's rule. This summary captures the essence of Mrichchhakatika, but the original play offers richness in character development, witty dialogue, and insightful commentary on human nature.
