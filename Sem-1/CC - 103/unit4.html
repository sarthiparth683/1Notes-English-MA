U R Anatmurthy: Samskara (Trans. A K Ramanujan)-1965

    • Setting the Stage: The story unfolds in Durvasapura, a small, decaying Brahmin village in India. The novel's title, "Samskara," carries multiple meanings, including "impression," " संस्कार (saṃskāra)" (ritual), and "completion." These meanings foreshadow the central themes of the narrative. The Disruption: The story begins with Praneshacharya, a learned Brahmin priest, caring for his ailing wife, Bhagirathi. Their peaceful routine is shattered by Chandri, a Dalit woman (considered an "untouchable" in the caste system) who arrives with news of Naranappa's sudden death. Naranappa, another Brahmin, was a rebel who defied traditional expectations. He rejected his wife, embraced a non-Brahminical lifestyle, and lived with Chandri. Despite his transgressions, the Brahmins never ostracized him. The Dilemma: Naranappa's death throws Durvasapura into turmoil. Praneshacharya informs Garuda, Naranappa's relative, of the death and the need to observe a ritual fast. The news spreads, revealing the villagers' anxieties about Naranappa's unorthodox life and their fear of social disorder. The Debate over Last Rites: A debate erupts regarding the performance of Naranappa's last rites. Naranappa's unconventional life, particularly his relationship with Chandri, creates uncertainty about whether he deserves the Brahmin death rituals. Praneshacharya, known for his wisdom, grapples with the dilemma.
