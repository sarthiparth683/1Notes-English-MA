• 1. John Dryden:- Absalom and Achitophel
  • John Dryden's poem "Absalom and Achitophel," published in 1681, is a scathing political satire disguised as a Biblical allegory.

  • Characters (Allegorical):
  • David: King Charles II of England
  • Absalom: James Scott, 1st Duke of Monmouth, Charles II's illegitimate son
  • Achitophel: Earl of Shaftesbury, a leading figure in the Exclusion Crisis Plot:
  • The poem uses the story of Absalom's rebellion against King David from the Old Testament as an allegory for the Exclusion Crisis in England (1679-1681). King Charles II (David) had a Catholic brother, James, Duke of York (the future James II). Parliament, fearing a Catholic ruler, attempted to exclude James from the line of succession in favor of Charles' illegitimate son, the Duke of Monmouth (Absalom). The poem portrays Charles II as a wise and benevolent king, Monmouth as a charming but ambitious rebel, and Shaftesbury (Achitophel) as a cunning and dangerous manipulator. Dryden, a royalist, uses satire to mock Monmouth's supporters and expose Shaftesbury's schemes.
