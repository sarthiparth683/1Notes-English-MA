UNIT-IV John Milton: Lycidas, Comus

John Milton's "Lycidas" is a pastoral elegy written to mourn the death of his friend Edward King, who drowned at a young age. Here's a breakdown of the poem's themes: Grief and Lamentation: The poem opens with the speaker, presumably Milton himself, expressing his deep sorrow for Lycidas (King's shepherd-like alias) and the injustice of his untimely demise. Nature's Sympathy: Milton depicts nature itself mourning Lycidas's death. The flowers bloom out of season, and even the mythological nymphs and shepherds grieve. Denouncing Corruption: The poem takes a sharp turn as the speaker criticizes the corrupt clergy of the time. He questions why God would allow a virtuous young man like Lycidas to perish while unworthy figures remain. Faith and Acceptance: Despite the initial anger, Milton finds solace in his faith. He imagines Lycidas dwelling in heaven among the saints and angels. The Power of Poetry: The poem underscores the significance of poetry as a way to immortalize the memory of the departed and find meaning in the face of loss. The speaker himself is revitalized by his act of mourning through verse. New Beginnings: The poem ends on a note of hope. Though deeply saddened, the speaker finds the strength to continue his own creative journey, seeking "fresh woods, and pastures new." "Lycidas" is a complex and beautiful poem that grapples with grief, faith, and the enduring power of art.
    John Milton's Comus Characters::-
  • The Lady: The central character, also referred to as the Lady Attendant, is unnamed. She represents virtue and chastity.
  • The Brothers: Two unnamed brothers who represent reason and steadfastness.
  • Comus: The antagonist, a sorcerer and the son of Bacchus (god of wine) and Circe (a sorceress in Greek mythology). He embodies temptation and indulgence.
  • Attendant Spirit: A benevolent spirit who guides and protects the Lady.
  • Sabrina: A nymph dwelling in a nearby river.
John Milton's Comus, also known as A Maske Presented at Ludlow Castle, is an allegory that explores the themes of good versus evil, temperance versus indulgence, and the importance of chastity. Here's a quick summary of the story: Two brothers travel with their younger sister (simply called "the Lady") through a dark forest. The Lady becomes separated from her brothers and encounters Comus, a deceptive sorcerer disguised as a helpful villager. Comus tempts the Lady with promises of pleasure and a magical potion, but she resists his advances thanks to her strong moral compass. He imprisons her in his enchanting palace, but the Lady remains resolute in her virtue. With the help of a magical Attendant Spirit, the brothers find their sister and free her from Comus's spell. A nymph named Sabrina helps break the spell completely, and the reunited siblings return home. Comus represents the temptations of sensuality and vice, while the Lady embodies virtue and self-control. The play celebrates the importance of resisting these temptations and staying true to one's morals.
