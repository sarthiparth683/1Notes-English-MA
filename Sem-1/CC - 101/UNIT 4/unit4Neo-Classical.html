Neo-Classical Age--Milton, Dryden, Pope, Dr. Johnson, Heroic Drama, Restoration Drama, Rise of the Novel, Periodicals, Socio-Political Scene.

  • Neo-Classical Age
  • Emphasis on Reason and Order: Neoclassical writers valued logic, clarity, and structure in their work. They often drew inspiration from the principles and forms of Ancient Greek and Roman literature.
  • Moral Purpose: Literature was seen as having a moral obligation to instruct and improve society. Satire and social commentary were common tools.
  • The Neo-Classical Age is often further divided into:
  • The Restoration Period (1660-1700): Followed the English Civil War and celebrated the return of the monarchy.
  • The Augustan Age (1700-1750): Considered the peak of Neoclassicism, named after the Roman Emperor Augustus.
  • The Age of Johnson (1750-1790): Marked a gradual shift towards Romanticism and a focus on individual expression.
  • John Milton
  • Emphasis on Reason and Order: His work, particularly Paradise Lost(Adam and Eve story), demonstrates a focus on structure, logic, and a sense of order in the universe, reflecting Neo-Classical values.
  • Classical Influence: Milton heavily drew inspiration from classical Greek and Roman writers like Homer and Virgil, utilizing epic forms, mythology, and themes in his own work. This aligns with the Neo-Classical emphasis on imitating and adapting classical models.
  • Moral Purpose: Milton's writing often aimed to convey moral lessons and explore themes of good versus evil, a characteristic shared by many Neo-Classical writers.
  • Blending of Styles: While he shared Neo-Classical traits, Milton also retained elements of the earlier Renaissance period, such as a focus on individual expression and a more emotional and imaginative approach. This unique blend sets him apart from purely Neo-Classical writers.
  • Religious Focus: Milton's work, particularly Paradise Lost, is deeply rooted in Christian theology and explores religious themes more profoundly than most Neo-Classical writers who often focused on secular themes and morality.
  • Key Works of Milton in this Context:
  • Paradise Lost: This epic poem, considered his masterpiece, exemplifies the Neo-Classical ideals of structure, order, and classical influence while also showcasing his unique blend of styles and religious depth.
  • John Dryden's
  • Reason and Order: He believed that reason should govern artistic expression and that literature should follow a clear, logical structure.
  • Imitation of Classical Models: He emphasized the importance of drawing inspiration from the works of ancient Greek and Roman writers, particularly in terms of form, style, and themes.
  • Decorum and Propriety: He stressed the importance of maintaining decorum, meaning characters and situations should behave in a way that befits their social standing and the overall tone of the work.
  • Critical Influence: Dryden is often referred to as the "Father of English Criticism." He established a critical tradition that emphasized:
  • Satire: He is particularly renowned for his mastery of verse satire, using wit and sharp wit to critique political and social issues. His most famous satires include "Absalom and Achitophel," which cleverly employed biblical allegory to comment on contemporary events.
  • Drama: Dryden was a prolific playwright, excelling in both comedy (like the witty "Marriage Ã  la Mode") and tragedy (like the acclaimed "All for Love"). These works adhered to Neoclassical principles of structure and character development.
  • Poetry: He perfected the use of the heroic couplet, a form where two consecutive rhyming lines create a distinct rhythm. He wrote various poems, including satires, religious pieces, and fables.
  • Translations: Dryden's skilled translations of classical works like Virgil's Aeneid further solidified his connection to the Greco-Roman literary tradition.
  • Satire: Absalom and Achitophel (1681):This masterfully crafted poem is considered Dryden's magnum opus. It uses an allegory based on the biblical story of Absalom's rebellion to satirize contemporary English politics, specifically the Exclusion Crisis.
  • Drama:All for Love : This acclaimed tragedy retells the story of Antony and Cleopatra, showcasing Dryden's mastery of dramatic structure and character development in the Neoclassical style.
  • Alexander Pope
  • Master of Form: Pope is renowned for his mastery of form, particularly the heroic couplet, a rhyming pair of lines in iambic pentameter. He elevated this form to new heights, using it with wit, precision, and elegance.
  • Famous Works: Some of his most celebrated works include:
  • An Essay on Criticism : This is a critical and philosophical poem, laying out principles of literary judgment and advocating for reason, good taste, and following the established rules of great writers like Homer and Virgil.
  • The Rape of the Lock : This mock-epic poem is a satirical masterpiece, cleverly using an exaggerated epic style to depict a trivial social incident - the theft of a curl of hair from a young woman.
  • The Dunciad (1728): This is a long and elaborate mock-epic poem satirizing the "dunces" of his time - bad poets, pedants, and pretentious critics.
  • Pope was also a notable translator, most famously rendering Homer's Iliad and Odyssey into English verse. He was a cultural icon of his era and his works continue to be admired for their wit, social commentary, and technical brilliance.
  • Dr. Johnson
  • Neoclassical Values: He strongly adhered to Neoclassical principles, emphasizing reason, order, balance, and clarity in writing.
  • Emphasis on Morality: His works often explored themes of morality, human nature, and the pursuit of knowledge.
  • Champion of the Classics: He greatly admired the works of ancient Greek and Roman writers, which influenced his own style and critical thinking.
  • Famous Works by Dr. Johnson:
  • A Dictionary of the English Language : This monumental work, compiled by Johnson over nine years, was a landmark achievement in defining and standardizing the English language.
  • The Lives of the Most Eminent English Poets : This collection of critical biographies on major English poets like Shakespeare, Milton, and Dryden cemented Johnson's reputation as a leading literary critic.
  • Rasselas : This philosophical novel, set in a fictional kingdom, explores themes of human happiness, the limitations of wealth and power, and the search for meaning in life.
  • London and The Vanity of Human Wishes : These satirical poems showcase Johnson's masterful use of language and his critique of social issues like poverty and ambition.
  • Heroic Drama in the Neo-Classical Age of English Literature:
  • What is Heroic Drama? A genre of plays popular during the Restoration period (roughly 1660s to 1670s) in England. Celebrated grand themes and epic stories, often drawing inspiration from mythology, history, or grand romances. Key characteristics: Verse Structure: Written primarily in heroic couplets, which are rhyming lines of iambic pentameter. Noble Heroes: Featured characters of almost superhuman virtue and courage, often facing internal conflicts between love, honor, and duty. Exalted Themes: Explored love, honor, loyalty, and other ideals in a grand and exaggerated manner.
  • Famous Works of Heroic Drama:
  • John Dryden: Considered the leading figure of Heroic Drama. Some of his famous works include: The Conquest of Granada: A two-part play showcasing themes of love, war, and political intrigue. All for Love : A retelling of the story of Antony and Cleopatra with a focus on their passionate love and tragic downfall.
  • Roger Boyle: Another prominent playwright of the genre. His notable work is: The Black Prince : Chronicles the historical figure of Edward, the Black Prince, and his military exploits.
  • The rise of Heroic Drama coincided with the Restoration of the monarchy after a period of political turmoil in England. It offered a sense of grandeur and escapism for the audience. While initially popular, Heroic Drama faced criticism for its artificiality and exaggerated elements. This led to its decline and the rise of satire plays that mocked its conventions.
  • Restoration Drama in the Neo-Classical Age of English Literature:
  • The Restoration Era refers to the period after King Charles II was restored to the English throne in 1660, following a period of Puritan rule under Oliver Cromwell.
  • This cultural shift brought a sense of celebration, hedonism, and a renewed interest in wit and social satire.
  • Characteristics of Restoration Drama:
  • Neoclassical Influences: Playwrights heavily adhered to principles inspired by ancient Greek and Roman dramas, emphasizing:
  • Order, logic, and restraint: Plots were tightly structured, with clear beginnings, middles, and ends.
  • The Three Unities: Dramas aimed to maintain unity of action, time (usually 24 hours), and place (a single location).
  • Witty dialogue: Plays were filled with clever wordplay, social commentary, and often bawdy humor.
  • Famous Playwrights and Works:
  • William Congreve: The Way of the World : Considered one of the finest comedies of manners, known for its witty dialogue and social satire.
  • William Wycherley: The Country Wife : A bawdy comedy exploring themes of sexual hypocrisy and social class.
  • Aphra Behn: The Rover: One of the few successful female playwrights of the era, known for her strong female characters and adventurous plots.
  • Rise of the Novel in the Neoclassical Age
  • Focus on Everyday Life: Novels explored the lives of ordinary people and their experiences in a realistic manner.
  • Complex Characters: Characters were more developed, with individual personalities, motivations, and flaws.
  • Plot-Driven Narratives: Novels followed clear plot structures, often with social commentary woven in.
  • Factors Contributing to its Rise
  • Growing Middle Class: An expanding middle class with increased literacy demanded stories that reflected their lives and concerns
  • Rise of Print Culture: Advancements in printing technology made books more affordable and accessible.
  • Emphasis on Reason and Observation: Neoclassical ideals of reason and observation aligned with the novel's focus on realistic portrayal.
  • Famous Neoclassical Novels and Novelists
  • Daniel Defoe : Robinson Crusoe : This is considered the first great English novel. It tells the story of a man stranded on a deserted island and his struggle for survival. Moll Flanders : This novel explores the life of a woman navigating the criminal underworld of London.
  • Samuel Richardson : Pamela, or Virtue Rewarded: This epistolary novel (written in letters) tells the story of a virtuous servant who resists her employer's advances. Clarissa, or the History of a Young Lady: This longer epistolary novel explores themes of love, betrayal, and social class.
  • Henry Fielding : Joseph Andrews : A comic novel that parodies Richardson's sentimental style. Tom Jones : Considered a masterpiece of the picaresque genre, it follows the adventures of a young man on his journey through life.
  • Laurence Sterne : Tristram Shandy : An experimental and unconventional novel known for its digressions, metafictional elements, and stream-of-consciousness style.
  • Rise of the Periodical Essay:
  • This era saw a significant rise in popularity of periodicals like journals, magazines, and newspapers.
  • These publications featured non-fictional prose essays published regularly, often mirroring the style and values of the Augustan Age in England.
  • The periodical essay focused on contemporary issues and morals, addressing broader trends and social observations rather than immediate events.
  • Famous Neoclassical Periodical Essays:
  • The Tatler : Founded by Richard Steele, it's considered the first major periodical essay publication. It offered witty observations on social life and manners.
  • The Spectator : Also founded by Steele, with Joseph Addison as a major contributor, it became even more influential than The Tatler. It covered a wider range of topics, including literature, philosophy, and morality, often using satire and humor.
  • The Rambler: Written solely by Samuel Johnson, it tackled literary criticism, morality, and social issues with a more serious and philosophical tone.
  • These periodicals played a vital role in shaping public opinion and fostering intellectual discourse during the Neoclassical Age.
  • They provided a platform for writers to engage with a wider audience and influence cultural trends.
  • The essays offered commentary and critiques on social behavior, fashion, literature, and the arts.
  • Socio-Political Scene in the Neo-Classical Age of English Literature:
  • Reason Over Religion: The Neo-Classical Age , also known as the Age of Enlightenment, saw a significant shift away from the religious turmoil that had dominated the previous century. People turned towards reason, logic, and scientific inquiry as the primary ways of understanding the world.
  • Social Commentary: Literature as a Tool: Neo-classical writers often used their works to comment on social and political issues. They satirized the aristocracy, criticized social hierarchies, and advocated for reason and logic in governance.
  • Famous Works and Authors:
  • John Dryden : Known for his satirical poems like "Absalom and Achitophel," which critiqued contemporary politics.
  • Alexander Pope : Famous for his mock-epic poem "The Rape of the Lock," a witty social satire.
  • Jonathan Swift : Author of the renowned satirical novel "Gulliver's Travels," a sharp critique of human nature and political systems.
  • Samuel Johnson : A prominent literary figure known for his essays and critical works, promoting reason and morality.
