Neo-Classical Age––Milton, Dryden, Pope, Dr. Johnson, Heroic Drama, Restoration Drama, Rise of the Novel, Periodicals, Socio-Political Scene.

The Neo-Classical Age, also known as the Age of Reason or the Enlightenment, was a period in European history from roughly the 1660s to the late 18th century. It was a time of great intellectual and cultural ferment, characterized by a belief in the power of reason and logic, a focus on order and balance, and an admiration for the art and literature of ancient Greece and Rome.
    Literary Figures of the Neo-Classical Age
  1. John Milton (1608-1674): Although technically pre-dating the Neo-Classical era, Milton's epic poem Paradise Lost (1667) is considered a bridge between the Renaissance and Neoclassical periods. It reflects the Neoclassical emphasis on order, reason, and morality, while also incorporating elements of classical mythology and epic tradition.
  2. John Dryden (1631-1700): A major figure in the Restoration period, Dryden is known for his satirical poems, plays, and critical essays. He championed the use of reason and wit in literature, and his work helped to establish the standards for Neoclassical writing.
  3. Alexander Pope (1688-1744): Perhaps the most famous Neoclassical poet, Pope is known for his polished and witty verse. His works, such as The Rape of the Lock (1712) and An Essay on Man (1733-34), explore themes of reason, order, and human nature.
  4. Samuel Johnson (1709-1784): A literary critic, biographer, essayist, lexicographer, and poet, Johnson is best known for his Dictionary of the English Language (1755), which helped to standardize the English language. He was also a leading figure in the London literary scene and a champion of Neoclassical values.
    Key Characteristics of Heroic Drama
  • Noble characters: Heroic dramas typically featured characters of high social standing, such as kings, queens, and nobles. These characters were often portrayed as being idealized figures, with superhuman strength, courage, and virtue.
  • Grand themes: Heroic dramas dealt with grand themes, such as love, honor, duty, and patriotism. The plays often explored the conflict between these themes and the challenges faced by the heroic characters.
  • Focus on reason and duty: Heroic dramas placed a strong emphasis on reason and duty. The characters were often torn between their personal desires and their sense of what is right.
    Famous Playwrights of Heroic Drama
  1. John Dryden (1631-1700): Dryden is considered the father of heroic drama. His most famous plays include The Conquest of Granada (1672) and Aureng-Zebe (1676).
  2. Thomas Otway (1652-1685): Otway is best known for his tragedy Venice Preserv'd (1682), which tells the story of two lovers who are brought down by jealousy and betrayal.
  3. Nathaniel Lee (c. 1653 – 1692): Lee's plays are known for their melodrama and sensationalism. His most famous work is Oedipus (1673), a retelling of the Greek myth.
    Characteristics of Restoration Drama
  • Wit and Satire: Restoration comedies are known for their witty dialogue, clever wordplay, and social satire. Playwrights poked fun at the manners and morals of the upper class, particularly their obsession with love, sex, and social status.
  • Intrigue and Deception: Plots often revolved around elaborate schemes of deception, mistaken identities, and sexual conquests. Playwrights delighted in creating situations where characters could outwit each other for amusement.
  • Focus on Conversation: Unlike earlier forms of drama, Restoration comedies placed a strong emphasis on witty conversation and repartee. Dialogue was often fast-paced and sparkling, showcasing the characters' intelligence and social graces.
  • Immorality and Cynicism: Restoration comedies reflected the cynicism and hedonism of the Restoration court. Many plays featured characters who were morally ambiguous and engaged in extramarital affairs and other scandalous behavior.
  • The “Comedy of Manners” : The most popular form of Restoration Comedy was the “Comedy of Manners,” which satirized the social customs and behaviors of the upper class. Playwrights like William Congreve and George Etherege were masters of this genre.
The Rise of the Novel: The novel emerged as a major literary form during the Neo-Classical Age. Early novels like Daniel Defoe's Robinson Crusoe (1719) and Samuel Richardson's Pamela (1740) explored themes of social mobility, individualism, and the rise of the middle class.
Periodicals: Periodicals, such as Joseph Addison and Richard Steele's The Tatler and The Spectator, became popular during the Neo-Classical Age. These publications offered essays, satire, and commentary on social and cultural issues.
Socio-Political Scene of the Neo-Classical Age The Neo-Classical Age was a time of great political and social change. The rise of absolute monarchy in some parts of Europe was challenged by the growth of democratic ideas. The Scientific Revolution led to a new emphasis on reason and logic, and the Enlightenment thinkers emphasized the importance of individual rights and liberties. Overall, the Neo-Classical Age was a period of great intellectual and cultural achievement. The writers and thinkers of this era helped to shape the modern world.
