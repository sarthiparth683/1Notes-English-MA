Jacobean Age––Drama, Poetry-Cavalier Lyricists, Metaphysical Poetry

The Jacobean Age (1603-1625) was a period of immense creativity in English literature, following the golden age of Elizabethan drama. It was a time of great change and uncertainty, as England grappled with new ideas about religion, politics, and science. This uncertainty is reflected in the literature of the period, which is often dark, complex, and preoccupied with themes of death, violence, and revenge.
Jacobean drama is known for its focus on these darker themes. Some of the most famous Jacobean playwrights include:
  • William Shakespeare (whose later plays, such as Hamlet, King Lear, Macbeth, and Othello, are considered to be Jacobean)
  • Ben Jonson, who wrote comedies such as Volpone and The Alchemist, as well as tragedies such as Sejanus
  • John Webster, whose plays, such as The Duchess of Malfi and The White Devil, are filled with violence and despair
  • Thomas Middleton, who wrote dark comedies such as The Changeling and A Trick to Catch the Old One
Poetry:- Jacobean poetry can be divided into two main movements: Cavalier poetry and Metaphysical poetry. Cavalier poetry is characterized by its lightheartedness, wit, and celebration of love and pleasure. Some of the most famous Cavalier poets include:
    Famous Cavalier Lyricists
  • Robert Herrick (1591-1674): Herrick is one of the most famous Cavalier poets. His poems are often witty and charming, and they celebrate the beauty of nature and the pleasures of love.
  • Thomas Carew (1594-1640): Carew was a courtier and diplomat who wrote about love, beauty, and pleasure. His poems are often more cynical than Herrick's, but they are still witty and entertaining.
  • Ben Jonson (1572-1637): Jonson is best known as a playwright, but he also wrote some fine Cavalier poems. His poems are often witty and satirical, and they offer a more realistic view of love than some of the other Cavalier poets.
Metaphysical poetry is a complex and intellectual style of poetry that emerged in England during the Jacobean Age (1603-1625). The term "metaphysical" was first applied to these poems in the 18th century by Samuel Johnson, who used it to describe the poets' exploration of philosophical and religious themes. Metaphysical poets are known for their use of elaborate metaphors, conceits (extended metaphors that compare seemingly unlike things), and paradoxes. Their poems are often dense and challenging, but they can also be very beautiful and moving.
  1. Wit: Metaphysical poets were highly skilled at using wit, which is a clever use of language that often involves surprise or paradox. Their poems are full of metaphors, and other figures of speech. Conceits: A conceit is an extended metaphor that compares two very different things in a surprising or unexpected way. Metaphysical poets often used conceits to explore complex ideas about love, death, and religion.
  2. Paradox: A paradox is a statement that seems to be contradictory, but may actually be true. Metaphysical poets often used paradoxes to explore the complexities of human experience. Famous Metaphysical Poets
  1. John Donne (1572-1631): Donne is considered the most important metaphysical poet. His poems are often passionate and intense, and they explore themes of love, death, and religion.
  2. George Herbert (1593-1633): Herbert was a religious poet who wrote about faith, doubt, and the nature of God. His poems are often witty and inventive, but they are also deeply serious.
  3. Andrew Marvell (1621-1678): Marvell was a later metaphysical poet who wrote about love, politics, and nature. His poems are known for their wit, beauty, and complexity.
