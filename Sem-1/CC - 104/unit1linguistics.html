  • Grammar and Vocabulary: Language consists of rules (grammar) and a set of words (vocabulary).
  • Spoken and Written Forms

  • 2. Language and Society, Language and Culture, Characteristics of human language.
  • Language and Society: Language varies significantly based on cultural context.Each influences the other.It focuses on how language reflects, shapes, and is shaped by various social factors.

  • 3. Difference between human language and animal communication.
  • Human Language:-Human language is a sophisticated(High class or level) system of communication that allows individuals to convey thoughts, feelings, ideas, and intentions. It goes beyond mere words and encompasses a broad range of symbolic elements, both spoken and written, that carry meaning.
  • Animal Communication:- In contrast, animal communication is more limited. It involves specific signals or behaviors that animals use to convey information to others of their species.
  • 4. What is linguistics(study of language)? Linguistics as a scientific study of language.
  • Linguistics is the scientific study of language. It delves into the various aspects of human language, including its structure, meaning, usage, and history. Linguists, the individuals who study linguistics, employ scientific methods to analyze and understand the complexities of language.
  • Phonetics(smallest unit of sound):-This field studies the physical properties of speech sounds, including how they are produced and perceived.
  • Phonology(study of sounds):This field examines the sound patterns of a language, including the rules that govern how sounds can be combined to form words.
  • Morphology(structure of words):-((m!w)) This area explores the structure of words, including how morphemes (the smallest meaningful units of language) are combined to form words.
  • Syntax(structure of sentence):-Syntax focuses on the rules and principles governing how words are arranged to form sentences.
  • Semantics(meaning of words):-This subfield investigates the meaning of words, phrases, and sentences.
  • Pragmatics:- How language is used w.r.t. speaker's intent and listener's background.

8. Diachronic and Synchronic linguistics.

1. Diachronic Linguistics (Historical Linguistics):
Focuses on the evolution and change of language over time.
Studies how languages develop, borrow elements from other languages, and transform their structure and vocabulary throughout history.
Analysis to reconstruct past language forms and trace their evolution.
2. Synchronic Linguistics ( Grammatical analysis,syntax,semantics...etc): Concentrates on describing the structure and function of a language at a specific point in time.
Aims to understand how a language works as a system at a particular moment.
Focuses on grammar, morphology, syntax, and semantics to analyze how elements within a language system interact and create meaning.
Ignores the historical development of the language, treating it as a static system for the chosen timeframe.

9. Syntagmatic and Paradigmatic relationships.

1. Syntagmatic Relationships (words are arranged in a specific order to convey meaning.):
Focus on the linear order and co-occurrence of words within a sentence or phrase.
Describe - how words combine and sequentially depend on each other to form grammatical and meaningful units.
Analyze the horizontal axis of language, where words are arranged in a specific order to convey meaning.
2. Paradigmatic Relationships(substitute or it's like synonyms): Focus on the substitutability of words within the same grammatical category or word class.
Explore how words with similar meanings or functions can be interchanged in a sentence while maintaining grammatical correctness, but potentially altering the meaning or emphasis.

10. Langue and Parole.

Langue(study the grammatical rules):-
Langue refers to the abstract, systematic rules and structures that govern a language. It encompasses the underlying principles of a signifying system, independent of any individual user.
It precedes individual usage and provides the foundation for meaningful communication.
Langue includes grammar, syntax, phonology, and vocabulary—the essential components that shape a language.
Example: When we study the grammatical rules of English or analyze its phonetic patterns, we are exploring the realm of langue.
Parole(actual speech acts, written texts, and utterances produced by individuals.):-
Parole refers to the concrete instances of language use—the actual speech acts, written texts, and utterances produced by individuals.
Concrete: Parole manifests in real-world communication, whether spoken or written.
Variability: Each individual’s parole may differ due to personal style, context, and intention.
Research Material: Linguists analyze parole to understand how langue operates in practice.
Example: When you engage in a conversation, write a poem, or deliver a speech, you are expressing parole.

11. Competence and Performance

Competence (Knowledge in any domain):
Competence refers to the knowledge, skills, and abilities that an individual possesses.
It represents the theoretical understanding and capacity to perform tasks within a specific domain.
Example:
When someone has linguistic competence, they understand the grammar, vocabulary,Phonology, Sementics, and syntax of a language. This knowledge enables them to communicate effectively.

Performance(Execution of Knowledge):-
Performance refers to how well an individual applies their competencies in real-world situations. It involves the actual execution of tasks or behaviors.
It encompasses actions, behaviors, and practical demonstrations.
Example:speaking, writing, Listening, Reading...etc..

12. Substance and Form.

Substance(phonemes and sound waves):-
In spoken language,it is sound waves, made up of phonemes (smallest units of sound that distinguish meaning).
In written language, it is like letters, punctuation marks, and characters.

Form(arrangement of syntax,words,sound,etc to convey meaning):-
It organizes the raw material (substance) into meaningful units.
It's how we arrange sounds or symbols to create words, sentences, and ultimately convey ideas.
Form includes things like grammar rules, syntax (sentence structure), and morphology (word formation).

13. Linguistic Sign and Symbol

Linguistic Sign(meaning in physical form(sound,text,etc) or assumptions):-
Introduced by Ferdinand de Saussure,
A linguistic sign is the fundamental unit of meaning in language.
It consists of two crucial elements:
Signifier: The physical form, either spoken (sound) or written (symbol), that represents something. (e.g., the word "cat" spoken or written)
Signified: The mental concept or meaning that the signifier refers to.
The relationship between signifier and signified is arbitrary, (meaning there's no inherent connection).

Symbol(representation in any form Symbols can be visual, auditory, or even tactile, not limited to the spoken or written forms of language):-
A symbol is a broader concept that encompass linguistic signs, but also extend beyond them.
The relationship between the symbol and what it represents can be: Arbitrary: eg. peace sign or mathematical symbol.
Symbols can be visual, auditory, or even tactile, not limited to the spoken or written forms of language.
