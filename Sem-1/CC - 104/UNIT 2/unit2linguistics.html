1.Levels of Linguistic Analysis: Phonetics, Phonology, Morphology, Syntax and Semantics;

Phonetics:Phonetics is the study of the physical properties of speech sounds. It examines how sounds are produced, transmitted, and perceived. Phonetics analyzes the airflow, lip position, and vocal cord vibration involved.

Phonology:Phonology focuses on the patterns of sounds within a language. It deals with the categorical organization of speech sounds. Example: it changes word meanings (e.g., “pat” vs. “bat”).

Morphology: Morphology studies the structure of words. It explores morphemes (the smallest units of meaning) and word formation processes. Example: The word “unhappiness” consists of three morphemes: “un-” , “happy,” and “-ness”.

Syntax: Syntax deals with the structure of sentences. It examines how words combine to form grammatical phrases and sentences. Example: The basic sentence structure in English is Subject-Verb-Object (e.g., “The girl read a book”).

Semantics: Semantics explores meaning in language. It investigates how words, phrases, and sentences convey messages.

Pragmatics: Pragmatics considers context in language use.
Example: “Could you pass the salt?” instead of “Give me the salt.”

2. Traditional approach to Linguistics.

Historical Linguistics:Investigates the evolution and changes in languages over time. Analyzes ancient texts, language families, and linguistic shifts. Example: Tracing the development of English from its Germanic roots.

Descriptive Linguistics:Describes and documents living languages. Example: Documenting endangered languages to preserve their knowledge. by :- Fieldwork, phonetic transcription, and grammar analysis.

Structural Linguistics: Examines the structure of language.Identifies patterns, rules, and components. Example: Analyzing sentence structures and word formations.

Generative Grammar: Proposes a universal grammar underlying all languages.Formal rules for sentence generation.

Sociolinguistics: Explores the social context of language use through,Surveys, interviews, and analyzing language variation.

Psycholinguistics: Studies the mental processes involved in language. Methods: Experiments, brain imaging, and language acquisition research. Example: Understanding how we comprehend sentences.

Applied Linguistics: Applied linguistic knowledge to real-world contexts. Language teaching, translation, and language policy. Example: Teaching English as a second language.

Comparative Linguistics:Compares languages to find commonalities and differences. Comparative analysis of vocabulary and grammar.

3. Behaviourist approach to Linguistics: Structural approach to Linguistics.

Behaviourist approach to Linguistics: The behaviourist approach provides insights into the role of habit formation and reinforcement in language learning ,capture the social and communicative functions of language use. . The behaviourist approach emphasizes observable behavior in language acquisition. Language learning occurs through associations between Stimulus-Response (S-R)(sounds, words) and responses (utterances).

Structural approach to Linguistics.The structural approach offers a framework for analyzing the internal organization of language.
Phonemes: speech sounds
Syntax: sentence structure
Semantics: meaning
Generative Grammar: generates grammatical sentences.

4. Cognitive approach to Linguistics.

Inseparability of Meaning and Form: Cognitive linguistics emphasizes that meaning and linguistic form are inseparable. It rejects the idea that meaning can be studied independently of how it is expressed in language. Unlike some other linguistic approaches, cognitive linguistics considers both the conceptual content (meaning) and the linguistic structure (form) as intertwined.

Meaning as Conceptualization: According to cognitive linguistics, meaning is essentially a process of conceptualization.Words, phrases, and sentences convey not just surface-level meanings but also deeper conceptual structures.

Grammar Shaped by Usage: Cognitive linguists argue that grammar is shaped by usage. They study how language is actually used in communication.This approach considers the functionality of linguistic structures and their role in conveying meaning.

Embodied Cognition:Cognitive linguistics embraces the idea of embodied cognition. It posits that our understanding of language is closely tied to our bodily experiences, sensory perceptions, and cultural context.
For example, metaphors like “time is money” or “love is a journey” are rooted in our physical experiences.

Construction Grammar: Construction grammar, focuses on constructions—form-meaning that occur in language. how constructions emerge from usage and contribute to our understanding of language.
